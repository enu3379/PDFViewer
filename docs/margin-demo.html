Human oversight in AI-assisted empirical research

M. Aoki · S. Park · D. Ferreira — Institute for Research Integrity

Abstract

Large language models can now draft full empirical studies, yet their outputs vary widely in rigor. We propose a lightweight oversight protocol in which every AI-generated analysis is screened by human experts before any publication decision (Figure 1). Each output receives a binary pass or fail judgement on three dimensions — feasibility, identification credibility, and output consistency — and the final label is decided by majority rule. Across 240 generated studies, screening removed 38% of outputs while preserving nearly all sound analyses.

1  Introduction

AI systems increasingly produce end-to-end research artifacts: a hypothesis, an estimation strategy, executable code, and a written interpretation. The convenience is real, but so is the risk that fluent text conceals a broken design. This paper asks how little human attention is enough to keep AI-assisted research trustworthy.

Figure 1 illustrates the oversight pipeline. A task specification is expanded by the model into a draft study, the analysis is executed against real data, and the packaged output is routed to expert screening. The three screening dimensions and the pass rule for each are summarized in Table 1.

2  Evaluation dimensions

Screening asks three questions. Feasibility: can the proposed analysis be run at all on the available data? Identification credibility: does the empirical strategy support a causal reading? Output consistency: do the reported numbers, tables, and narrative agree with one another?

Failures take recognizable forms. A study may request variables that do not exist, or apply difference-in-differences without a valid comparison group, or report coefficients that contradict its own tables. Table 1 lists the pass criterion reviewers apply on each dimension.

3  Evaluation procedure

As shown in Figure 2, each generated output is judged independently by three expert reviewers. Every reviewer returns a pass or fail judgement on all three dimensions, and the final label on each dimension follows majority rule — at least two of three.

Reviewers never see one another's judgements. Inter-rater agreement is summarized with mean pairwise Cohen's κ, computed per dimension and pooled across the corpus.

4  Results

The protocol in Figure 2 removed 91 of 240 generated studies. Most rejections came from identification credibility, matching the pattern anticipated in Table 1: fluent designs with no credible counterfactual.

Agreement was substantial. Pooled κ reached 0.74 for feasibility, 0.61 for identification credibility, and 0.69 for output consistency — high enough that a short expert screen is reliable to act on.

5  Conclusion

A few minutes of structured human attention filters most unsound AI-generated research while keeping the sound majority. Oversight, not authorship, may be the scarce human contribution to machine-drafted science.
